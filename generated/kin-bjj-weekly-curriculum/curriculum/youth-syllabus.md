# Youth Program Syllabus

This syllabus maps the youth weekly sequence to its cycle, theme, description, and main goal.

| Week | Cycle | Theme | Description | Main Goal |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 01 | defensive | Early Escape Frames From Mount And Side Control | Early-stage escape structure from mount and side control. | Build calm survival habits, frames, and first-movement escapes from major pins. |
| 02 | defensive | Late Escape Chains And Safe Guard Recovery | Middle and late-stage escape chains from mount, knee on belly, and heavy side control. | Teach students how to recover when the first escape fails and pressure is already established. |
| 03 | offensive | Closed Guard Control And Simple Sweeps | Closed guard posture breaking and hip-bump style sweep threats. | Introduce closed guard as a controlled offensive platform with posture breaking and simple sweep entries. |
| 04 | offensive | Closed Guard Submission Chains | Closed guard armbar and triangle pathway with safe finishing mechanics. | Connect closed guard control to one clean submission chain that matches youth safety standards. |
| 05 | defensive | Open Guard Retention And Knee-Elbow Recovery | Open guard retention, hip turns, and knee-elbow recovery against passing pressure. | Show students how to keep their feet and knees in front of the passer and recover structure early. |
| 06 | defensive | Seated And De La Riva Recovery Reactions | Recovering seated guard and De La Riva hooks after the passer clears the first line. | Build defensive reactions from seated and De La Riva style guard positions when grips or hooks break down. |
| 07 | offensive | Headquarters Passing And Knee-Cut Entry | Headquarters posture, leg stapling, and knee-cut passing. | Introduce structured top passing through headquarters and knee-cut style entries. |
| 08 | offensive | Toreando And Leg-Drag Passing Chains | Passing around the legs with toreando and leg-drag concepts. | Expand offensive passing into mobile routes that still finish with real control. |
| 09 | defensive | Side Control Escape Layers And Kesa Survival | Escapes from side control, kesa gatame, and transition pressure. | Prepare students for heavy side control variations, including kesa and reverse-kesa style pressure. |
| 10 | defensive | Mount And Knee-On-Belly Escape Chains | Defensive reactions from mount and knee on belly into half guard or full guard recovery. | Link mount escapes and knee-on-belly escapes into repeatable reactions instead of isolated moves. |
| 11 | offensive | Side Control Control To Americana | Side control pressure, far-side isolation, and americana mechanics. | Teach top players how to secure side control before attacking one high-percentage youth-safe submission. |
| 12 | offensive | Mount Progression To Armbar And Collar Attack | Mount control, high mount progression, and armbar or collar-based attack entries. | Build mount progression from control into one clear attack pathway and one transition option. |
| 13 | defensive | Turtle Posture, Sit-Outs, And Front-Headlock Defense | Turtle structure, sit-outs, and defensive reactions to front-headlock pressure. | Use turtle as a dynamic defensive station instead of a hiding position. |
| 14 | defensive | Back Survival, Hand Fighting, And Rear-Escape Chains | Back defense, two-on-one hand fighting, and escaping to safe top or guard positions. | Give students a structured method for surviving the back and removing hooks or choking control. |
| 15 | offensive | Rear Mount Control To Strangle Finish | Rear mount control, hook management, and clean rear-strangle mechanics. | Teach secure rear control and one disciplined strangle pathway that fits youth safety rules. |
| 16 | offensive | North-South Control And Transition Attacks | North-south control, spin transitions, and submission entry timing. | Show students how north-south can be a controlling transition hub rather than just a pass-through position. |
| 17 | defensive | Grip Fighting, Stance, And Sprawl Reactions | Grip fighting, stance discipline, level-change awareness, and sprawls. | Build standing defense through posture, inside position, and first-contact reactions. |
| 18 | defensive | Takedown Counters To Safe Top Entries | Countering common takedown attempts and landing in stable top control. | Move from pure takedown defense into safe top-position conversions. |
| 19 | offensive | Inside Reaps, Ouchi, And Osoto To Top Control | Inside trips and outer reaps that land directly into side control or scarf-hold pressure. | Connect basic off-balancing throws and trips to immediate top control themes. |
| 20 | offensive | Double Leg And Body-Lock Entries To Passing Finish | Double leg or body-lock entry leading to stable top position and first pass. | Blend wrestling-style takedown entries with immediate passing and settling behavior on the ground. |
| 21 | defensive | Armbar And Triangle Defense | Defensive posture and escape routes against armbar and triangle attempts. | Teach submission awareness without panic by focusing on posture, stack direction, and early exits. |
| 22 | defensive | Choke Defense, Tap Awareness, And Reset Reactions | Defending rear strangles, cross-collar style threats, and recovering composure after a failed defense. | Finish the defensive cycle by reinforcing hand fighting, neck safety, and calm reset behavior under submission pressure. |
| 23 | self-defense | Standing Self-Defense: Distance, Base, And Clinch Safety | Standing self-defense reactions, posture, breakfalls, and safe disengagement. | Introduce standing self-defense through posture, distance management, breakfalls, and simple clinch exits. |
| 24 | self-defense | Ground Self-Defense: Pins, Escapes, And Technical Stand-Up | Ground self-defense priorities: protect, frame, escape, stand, and create space. | Finish the curriculum by connecting ground safety, pin survival, technical stand-up, and disengagement decisions. |
