# Class Structure Principles

## Definition
Class structure is treated as a delivery system that must be deliberate, repeatable, and consistent if technical curriculum is to produce stable results.

## Detailed Notes
- The sources treat class structure as a learning system, not just a timetable. If the room is disorganized, even strong technical content will not stick consistently.
- Motivation, challenge, and fun are all named as drivers of learning quality, which means structure should support engagement rather than feel like a rigid military schedule.
- Coaches are expected to be firm enough to maintain boundaries and pace, but not so rigid that the class loses energy or adaptability.
- Assistant coaches matter because they preserve flow: they can redirect behavior, help confused students, and keep the head instructor focused on teaching.
- Positive reinforcement is not presented as a soft extra. It is part of the actual classroom-management model.

## Operational Implications
- A coach should think about timing, transitions, attention span, and behavior management as part of lesson quality.
- A systematic class structure also makes it easier to deliver the same standard across different instructors.

## Constraints or Safety Notes
- The sources give principles and examples, but they do not create one unchangeable script for every age group or room condition.
- Younger groups need more resets, shorter explanations, and tighter behavior management than older cohorts.

## Gaps
- The sources provide principles and examples rather than a single unchangeable SOP for every age group.
