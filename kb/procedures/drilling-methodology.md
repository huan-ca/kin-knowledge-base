# Drilling Methodology

## Definition
Drilling is framed as the bridge between technique demonstration and open sparring, and it should be specific, progressive, and goal-oriented.

## Detailed Notes
- The sources describe drilling as the bridge between “I saw the move” and “I can apply the move live.” That makes drilling one of the most important translation layers in the room.
- Solo drills help build basic movement literacy and can fit naturally into warm-ups.
- Call-out drills create recognition and reaction speed by forcing a response to a named cue from a known position.
- Pocket drills narrow the exchange to a small tactical problem so the students cannot hide from the target skill.
- Objective drills attach a measurable outcome such as a score, a submission, or a clean escape.
- Reset drills let coaches keep the exchange focused by returning students to the problem after the key outcome is reached.
- Progressive resistance is central to the model. Students should not stay forever at zero resistance, but they also should not jump straight into chaotic sparring.

## Operational Implications
- Coaches can choose the drill family based on the job of the lesson: patterning, timing, troubleshooting, reaction speed, or live conversion.
- This also means a good lesson plan should name the drill structure, not just the technique being drilled.

## Gaps
- The source gives baseline drill families, but not a complete drill catalog for every curriculum theme.
