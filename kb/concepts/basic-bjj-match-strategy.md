# Basic BJJ Match Strategy

## Definition
Students should be taught the logic of a BJJ match, not only isolated techniques.

## Detailed Notes
- The source treats strategy as a sequence of connected decisions rather than a pile of unrelated techniques.
- A simple top-game lane runs from stance and grip fighting into a takedown, then into lower-body control, a pass, stronger chest-to-chest control, positional advancement, and only then a submission attempt.
- A parallel bottom-game lane begins with grip fighting or guard engagement, then posture disruption, off-balancing or sweeping, and finally submissions once the opponent’s structure is compromised.
- The strategic teaching value is that students can start to understand why certain positions matter in a match and why one technical decision should lead to the next.

## Operational Implications
- Coaches can use this page to frame weekly themes as connected match sequences instead of as disconnected stand-up and ground modules.
- This also supports the curriculum brief’s demand that takedowns, ground work, and submissions flow together in one theme.

## Examples or Variants
- A takedown week might emphasize a clean landing into side control before any attack language appears.
- A guard week might emphasize posture breaking and sweep timing before talking about armbar or triangle chains.

## Gaps
- The source emphasizes strategic understanding, but individual tournament strategies still need event-specific adaptation.
