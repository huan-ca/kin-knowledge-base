# Four Layers of BJJ Skill Development

## Definition
The four-layer model describes BJJ development through techniques, muscle memory, principles, and intuition rather than treating progress as technique accumulation alone.

## Detailed Notes
- Techniques give beginners a starting map. They are the easiest layer to teach directly because they can be demonstrated, copied, and remembered as sequences.
- Muscle memory is what turns a remembered sequence into something that still works under pressure, fatigue, and resistance. The source explicitly ties this to drilling, positional work, and live rolling.
- Principles are the layer that connects isolated techniques into a system. Instead of memorizing one pass, a student can understand ideas like posture, connection, frames, pressure, or angle.
- Intuition is the long-horizon layer. It is what lets a grappler recognize openings and timing cues without consciously narrating every step.
- The article’s strongest claim is that all four layers reinforce one another. Technique without feel is brittle, repetition without principles is narrow, and intuition without clear concepts can plateau.

## Operational Implications
- Coaches can use this model to diagnose why a student is stalled: sometimes the issue is not more techniques, but more repetition, better principles, or more live context.
- It also supports lesson design that cycles between demonstration, drill, conceptual explanation, and live application.

## Gaps
- The source presents a conceptual model, but it does not prescribe exact programming ratios or lesson allocations for each layer.
