# Guard Framework

## Definition
Guard is treated as both a defensive shield and an offensive platform organized by engagement, secure or control, retention or recovery, sweeps, and submissions.

## Detailed Notes
- The compendium presents guard as both shield and weapon. It is not just a stalled bottom position; it is a structured platform for preventing passes and creating offense.
- The source covers multiple guard families, including closed guard, open guard, de la riva or reverse de la riva, worm guard, butterfly or seated guard, and half guard.
- A useful structural distinction in the source is between offensive cycles and defensive cycles. Offensively, guard should generate sweeps and submissions. Defensively, it should focus on retention and recovery.
- Sweeps are framed through leverage, post removal, and creating weak corners. Submissions are supposed to follow posture disruption and control rather than appear from nowhere.

## Examples or Variants
- Closed guard works well as an early example of posture breaking and simple sweep or submission chains.
- De la Riva and worm-style material show a more advanced retention and off-balancing lane.
- Butterfly and seated guard emphasize timing, angle, and the ability to elevate or redirect weight.

## Gaps
- The source is rich, but a final curriculum still needs to decide which guard families to emphasize first for each age or level.
